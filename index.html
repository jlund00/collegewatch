Background

What's Actually Happening — and Why It Matters

Community colleges serve the students most likely to be left behind if their institution fails. Understanding the full picture requires looking back a decade.

📉
-19%
Enrollment Lost Since 2014

From 5.51 million students in 2014 to 4.46 million in 2024. The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated a decline that had already been underway for years. State per-pupil funding follows enrollment, which means every lost student is also lost revenue — while utilities, faculty contracts, and building maintenance costs stay the same.

💸
59.7%
Colleges Classified Distressed

524 of 878 colleges have a spending-to-revenue ratio above 0.90 — meaning they're spending 90+ cents for every dollar they earn. These colleges have little financial cushion. A single unexpected cost or enrollment dip can push them into deficit. This is not a regional problem: distressed colleges appear in every state.

🏛️
AACC
The Client Organization

The American Association of Community Colleges represents over 900 public two-year institutions nationwide. It is the primary national advocacy organization for the sector. The AACC needed a standardized tool that any state higher education board can run annually using free federal data — no new reporting, no new cost, updated every year automatically when IPEDS releases new numbers.

10-Year Timeline
14
2014 — Deliberate Baseline
31.1% of colleges already in deficit
EdOptima chose 2014 as a deliberate 10-year baseline — not because it's the only year available, but because comparing where the sector stood a decade ago to where it stands today is the most compelling way to demonstrate that this is a structural, long-term problem. 273 colleges were spending more than they earned. Enrollment had already begun its long decline.
18
2018 — The Recovery That Wasn't
Still 31.2% — four years into economic growth
By 2018, U.S. unemployment was near historic lows and the economy had been growing for years. The community college at-risk rate barely moved. This confirmed the problem was structural — not cyclical. Enrollment kept falling. Cost structures did not adjust. The sector was heading toward a slow-motion crisis.
22
2022 — The Relief Illusion
9.6% at-risk — but only because of $14B in federal money
The HEERF (Higher Education Emergency Relief Fund) injected $14 billion into community colleges. The at-risk rate dropped to 9.6% — its lowest point in our dataset. Many state boards interpreted this as recovery. It was not. It was a painkiller. The underlying illness — falling enrollment, rising costs — was unchanged. When the relief expired, the numbers came back.
24
2024 — Rising Again
13.9% and projected to reach 18.4% by 2027
With HEERF funds fully depleted, the at-risk rate has climbed to 13.9%. Institution-level projections applying each college's own enrollment trend forward with 2% annual expense growth project 147 colleges in deficit spending by 2027 — 36 more than today. CollegeWatch was built to intervene before these projections become reality.

Methodology

How the Scoring Model Works

Three steps: normalize the inputs, calculate the risk probability, assign a state board action. Built on 878 real IPEDS colleges.

Step 01 — Normalize

All inputs are standardized

Each variable is centered and scaled using the mean and standard deviation from the real 878-college IPEDS dataset — matching the scale() transformation used in R Studio.

ner = (er − 0.90) / 0.12
nec = (−ec) / 15.0
nrps = (9500 − rps) / 2500

Normalization ensures no single variable dominates because of its scale. The spending ratio has the largest real-world weight (2.2×); enrollment change is secondary (0.85×); revenue per student is tertiary (0.65×).

Step 02 — Score

Risk probability is calculated

A logistic model calibrated against real k-NN cluster outcomes from the R analysis converts the normalized inputs into a 0–100% at-risk probability.

logit = 2.2·ner + 0.85·nec
+ 0.65·nrps + 0.15
score = sigmoid(logit) × 100

Hard rules enforce the actual at_risk definition from the R model: if spending ratio exceeds 1.0 AND enrollment is falling more than 5%, the minimum score is raised to 70%. If ratio exceeds 1.0 AND enrollment falls more than 10%, minimum is 82%.

Step 03 — Prescribe

Score maps to a state board action

Each college's score falls into one of four tiers. Each tier has a specific, actionable instruction for state higher education boards.

≥ 75% → ACT NOW
50–74% → INTERVENE
30–49% → WATCH
0–29% → STABLE

These thresholds were set based on the cluster profiles from the k-Means analysis. The Distressed cluster (524 colleges, avg spending ratio 0.941) typically scores 50–75%. The Act Now fringe (103 colleges with exp_ratio >1.0) scores 75%+.
